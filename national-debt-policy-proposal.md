# The Fiscal Capacity and Stability Act of 2026
## A Realistic, Bipartisan-Adjacent Proposal to Stabilize the U.S. National Debt

**Prepared as a policy white paper**  
**Date: June 2026**  
**Context**: Addresses the national debt at approximately $39.2 trillion (early June 2026), with annual deficits near $1.9 trillion and debt held by the public exceeding 100% of GDP and rising under current law projections.

---

## Executive Summary

The United States faces persistent large deficits—$1.78 trillion in FY2025 and projected at $1.9 trillion for FY2026—driving the national debt to roughly $39.2 trillion. Mandatory spending (primarily Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid), net interest costs (already ~14% of the budget and climbing), and prior policy choices have created a structural imbalance. Without action, CBO projects debt held by the public reaching 120% of GDP by the mid-2030s, with deficits widening further.

This proposal, the **Fiscal Capacity and Stability Act of 2026**, offers a realistic, legislatively viable path to bend the curve. It does not promise magical elimination of the debt or immediate balance. Instead, it stabilizes debt-to-GDP through a combination of:

- **Growth as the primary engine** (energy dominance, permitting reform, pro-investment tax policy, and skills/innovation incentives).
- **Revenue from tariffs** (building on recent gains, dedicated in part to deficit reduction).
- **Aggressive but targeted efficiency and waste reduction** (statutory DOGE 2.0 with real enforcement tools).
- **Discretionary restraint** focused on non-defense overhead and duplicative programs.
- **Conditional reforms to mandatory programs** (state flexibility, work requirements, technical efficiencies—protecting current beneficiaries).
- **Strong transparency, classification, and enforcement mechanisms** (a "Fiscal Ledger," triggers tied to the debt limit, and an independent long-term commission for entitlements).

**Guiding framework** draws from rigorous structural analysis: honest classification of "Core" (protected stability elements) versus "Conditional" (adjustable) items; explicit capacity partitioning of the fiscal space; clean "observer collapse" via measurable savings and transparency rather than optimistic claims; multiple typed tools rather than reliance on any single lever; named assumptions and validation requirements; and growth treated as the exceptional high-leverage layer.

**Projected impact (illustrative, conventional scoring + dynamic effects)**: Several trillion dollars in deficit reduction over 10 years relative to current-law baselines, combined with growth effects sufficient to stabilize or slowly reduce debt/GDP (targeting a path toward 3% of GDP deficits). This aligns with goals discussed in policy circles while remaining achievable under current political realities.

The package is designed to pass a Republican-led Congress via reconciliation or must-pass vehicles (debt limit, appropriations, continuing resolutions) and receive President Trump's signature. It emphasizes America First priorities: unleashing domestic energy and manufacturing, using tariffs for revenue and reciprocity, cutting waste and "deep state" overhead, increasing defense and border security, protecting Social Security and Medicare for current retirees, and delivering growth that outpaces debt accumulation.

## 1. The Current Situation

As of early June 2026, total gross federal debt stands at approximately $39.2 trillion. Debt held by the public is near or above 101% of GDP in baselines and projected to climb significantly. 

FY2025 closed with a $1.78 trillion deficit (outlays $7.01 trillion against revenues of $5.23 trillion). FY2026 projections center around $1.85–1.9 trillion. Net interest payments exceed $1 trillion annually and are rising rapidly, crowding out other priorities.

**Major spending composition (approximate recent shares)**:
- Social Security: ~22%
- Net interest: ~14%
- Medicare: ~14%
- Other health programs: ~14%
- National defense: ~13%
- Income security and veterans benefits: significant additional shares

Mandatory spending overall accounts for roughly 59% of the budget, discretionary ~27%, and net interest the balance. Recent legislation (including the 2025 "One Big Beautiful Bill Act") added substantial costs through tax provisions and select spending, partially offset by higher tariff revenues (projected multi-trillion-dollar help over the decade in some analyses) and limited rescissions.

Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) efforts produced meaningful but limited savings (low billions realized through workforce reductions and targeted actions), far below early ambitious targets. Long-term drivers remain demographics (aging population increasing entitlement costs) and interest compounding.

CBO baselines show deficits growing to $3 trillion+ by 2036 absent policy changes, with debt-to-GDP exceeding prior historical peaks outside of world wars.

A credible 3% of GDP deficit target would require roughly $10 trillion in cumulative deficit reduction over a decade in some frameworks, achievable only through a balanced mix of restraint, revenue measures, and growth rather than any single approach.

Past efforts have suffered from over-optimism, hidden assumptions, lack of enforcement, and failure to protect core political and social stability while adjusting conditional elements.

## 2. Guiding Principles: A Structural, Ledger-Based Framework

This proposal is informed by principles of rigorous structural analysis (classification of claims, capacity partitioning, observer mechanisms, validation, and layered effective descriptions). Applied to fiscal policy, they yield the following operational rules:

**A. Honest Classification (Core Ledger vs. Conditional Layers)**  
Every major program, tax provision, and revenue source must be explicitly classified in an annual "National Fiscal Ledger":
- **Core**: Protected elements essential for stability and current commitments (e.g., Social Security and Medicare benefits for existing retirees and near-retirees; core national defense; veterans' earned benefits). Changes here are minimal and phased for future cohorts only.
- **Conditional**: Adjustable items subject to review, efficiency, or reform (e.g., administrative overhead, duplicative programs, certain discretionary accounts, Medicaid growth parameters, future entitlement expansions).
- **Growth-Dependent / Conjectural**: Items whose fiscal impact relies on economic performance or behavioral responses (scored with explicit sensitivity bands and assumptions).

This prevents conflation of solid baselines with optimistic projections.

**B. Capacity Partition and Balance**  
The federal "capacity" (tax base, GDP, political will, and borrowing headroom) must be explicitly partitioned and balanced across priorities rather than assumed to accommodate all demands. Weights or guardrails (e.g., mandatory spending as a share of outlays or GDP, interest as a share of the budget, total non-interest outlays targets) are set using external data (economic multipliers, demographic realities) rather than circular reasoning. No single lever is treated as a universal "maximizer."

**C. Clean Observer Collapse and Shadow Management**  
Only measurable, realized savings and revenues ("collapsed" into the observable ledger) count toward targets. "Shadow" elements—unfunded liabilities, optimistic dynamic scoring without validation, off-balance-sheet commitments, or unverified waste claims—continue to exert pressure even if sequestered. Reforms must strengthen transparency, scoring rules, and enforcement ("projector") to reduce leakage and contamination of the observable fiscal picture.

**D. Typed Tools, Not a Single Operator**  
Different problems require different instruments:
- Tariffs and trade policy for boundary/revenue and reciprocity.
- Statutory efficiency and rescission authority for waste reduction.
- Permitting and regulatory reform for the growth layer.
- Parametric and incentive changes for conditional mandatory programs.
- Triggers and independent review for enforcement and long-term solvency.
No one tool (e.g., efficiency alone or growth alone) is expected to solve the full structure.

**E. Named Assumptions, Validation, and Falsifiability**  
All significant projections carry explicit, named assumptions (e.g., real GDP growth of X% under the growth plank; net tariff revenue after economic and retaliation effects; enforcement fidelity rates). An annual independent validation process (GAO/CBO-led with external input) reviews progress against the ledger, retires contradicted assumptions, and updates open problems. Historical contributions of prior laws are archived for accountability.

**F. Growth as the Exceptional High-Leverage Layer**  
While restraint and revenue are essential, sustainable stabilization ultimately requires real economic growth that raises the denominator (GDP) and revenues faster than debt accumulation. Policies must prioritize energy production, domestic manufacturing, innovation, and investment incentives as the primary "exceptional" mechanism, consistent with historical episodes where growth contributed materially to debt dynamics.

**G. Political and Incentive Realism (Fixed Points and Admitted Flows)**  
Reforms must create stable political and economic equilibria ("observer fixed points"). This means protecting current beneficiaries to maintain social and political stability, aligning incentives (e.g., state skin-in-the-game for Medicaid), and using enforcement triggers rather than relying solely on goodwill. "Admitted" trajectories respect lower-level constraints (political feasibility, economic feedbacks, legal realities).

These principles produce a package that is disciplined yet pragmatic—aggressive on waste and growth, protective of core commitments, and equipped with self-correcting mechanisms.

## 3. The Proposal: Key Provisions

The Fiscal Capacity and Stability Act would be enacted through available legislative vehicles (reconciliation for tax/revenue/spending changes where possible; debt limit legislation; appropriations measures). It includes the following pillars.

### Pillar 1: National Fiscal Ledger and Transparency Requirements
- Mandate an annual joint CBO/OMB/GAO "National Fiscal Ledger" report classifying all major budget functions and tax expenditures as Core, Conditional, or Growth-Dependent, with explicit assumptions, sensitivity analysis, and 10-year projections.
- Require dynamic scoring with risk bands and independent validation of key assumptions.
- Establish a public "Fiscal History Archive" documenting the contribution of major prior laws (including recent reconciliation measures) to the current debt path.
- Create a small independent Fiscal Review Panel (GAO-supported) to audit progress and flag deviations.

This implements rigorous classification and validation.

### Pillar 2: Accelerate Growth and Secure Revenue (The Exceptional Layer)
- **Tax Policy**: Extend or make permanent key pro-growth and investment provisions from recent tax law (full expensing, lower individual and pass-through rates where politically viable) with design choices that limit static costs. Include targeted base-broadening pay-fors only as needed for internal scoring (avoid broad rate hikes).
- **Energy and Permitting Reform**: Comprehensive reform to expedite permitting for energy (all sources, with emphasis on domestic production and nuclear), manufacturing, infrastructure, and critical technology projects. Include streamlined environmental reviews with deadlines and judicial reforms. This is a high-multiplier growth measure with job and supply benefits.
- **Workforce and Innovation**: Expand skills/apprenticeship tax credits, domestic manufacturing incentives, and high-skill legal immigration pathways tied to economic needs.
- **Tariffs and Customs**: Codify a strategic tariff framework emphasizing reciprocity and revenue generation. Dedicate a formulaic portion of incremental customs duties (above baseline) explicitly to deficit reduction or debt service mitigation. Monitor and adjust for net economic effects.

These measures treat growth as the primary solver while generating visible revenue without new taxes on American workers and businesses.

### Pillar 3: Statutory Efficiency, Waste Elimination, and DOGE 2.0
- Grant enhanced expedited rescission authority (building directly on the 2025 Rescissions Act precedent) allowing the President and Congress to target identified waste, fraud, duplicative programs, and low-performing initiatives with accelerated procedures and limited judicial review.
- Establish a statutory regulatory budget: net-zero or strict "2-for-1" requirements for major new rules' compliance costs, with independent accounting.
- Fund and empower an ongoing efficiency initiative with measurable targets, clawback authority for unachieved savings, workforce reforms (attrition, buyouts, and restructuring where justified), and program sunsets/reviews.
- Aggressively target improper payments (known high-error areas in health, unemployment insurance, and other programs) with recovery goals in the tens to hundreds of billions over the decade.
- Focus non-defense discretionary restraint on administrative overhead, certain grant programs, and initiatives identified as low-priority (consistent with recent administration requests and enacted 2026 levels), while protecting or increasing defense, border security, and veterans programs.

This delivers real, auditable savings rather than aspirational targets.

### Pillar 4: Targeted Reforms to Conditional Spending
- **Discretionary**: Enact or extend caps/growth limits on non-defense base discretionary spending (with exemptions or offsets for defense and border priorities). Build on restraint already reflected in recent cycles.
- **Medicaid**: Introduce greater state flexibility (enhanced block grant or per-capita cap options) paired with work requirements for able-bodied, non-elderly, non-disabled adults. This aligns incentives without altering core eligibility for vulnerable populations.
- **Medicare**: Pursue technical and value-based reforms (site-neutral payments, further expansion of negotiation where consistent with access goals, premium or cost-sharing adjustments for higher-income beneficiaries in new or future cohorts). No changes to benefits or eligibility for current retirees.
- Additional savings drawn from CBO-style options in "other mandatory" areas (veterans programs, federal employee benefits, means-tested programs, premiums/fees) where they align with efficiency and integrity goals.

These target growth in conditional areas while preserving the Core.

### Pillar 5: Enforcement, Triggers, and Debt Limit Linkage
- Tie any required debt limit increase to enactment of this package plus automatic, modest, targeted sequesters or review triggers if primary deficits or debt-to-GDP exceed specified thresholds (with Core programs and defense largely exempt).
- Require that a share of tariff revenues and realized efficiency savings be applied to deficit reduction.
- Include performance metrics, reporting, and mid-course correction requirements tied to the annual Ledger.

This creates stable enforcement "fixed points."

### Pillar 6: Long-Term Entitlement Sustainability Commission
- Establish a bipartisan, expert commission (including actuaries, economists, and trustees representatives) tasked with developing parametric recommendations for Social Security and Medicare solvency.
- Recommendations would focus on future cohorts (e.g., gradual longevity-indexed adjustments to retirement age or COLAs, premium support options) and carry fast-track legislative consideration (up-or-down vote, limited amendments).
- This addresses the shadow of long-term demographic pressures without immediate changes to current beneficiaries.

## 4. Estimated Fiscal Impact

Using conventional scoring baselines (CBO-style) augmented by plausible dynamic effects:

- **Restraint and Efficiency (discretionary caps, rescissions, improper payments, regulatory budget, targeted mandatory technical reforms)**: Low-to-mid trillions over 10 years.
- **Tariff/customs and base maintenance**: Hundreds of billions to low trillions in incremental revenue (building on already-material recent gains).
- **Growth effects** (permitting/energy, tax design, investment incentives): The swing factor—plausible 0.2–0.5%+ sustained real GDP boost contributing meaningfully to revenues and the debt ratio.
- **Net**: Multi-trillion conventional deficit reduction over the decade, plus growth dividend sufficient (under stated assumptions) to stabilize debt/GDP and move toward a 3% of GDP deficit trajectory by the mid-2030s. Interest costs would be materially lower than baseline due to smaller debt stock and stronger economy.

Exact figures depend on final legislative text and official scoring. The structure is adjustable in negotiation while preserving the core ledger discipline and growth emphasis. Savings draw from menus of options similar to those published by CBO in its periodic "Options for Reducing the Deficit" volumes (e.g., means-testing adjustments, program integrity, discretionary reductions, and other mandatory reforms).

Compared to current-law worsening, this package would represent a substantial improvement in the fiscal trajectory without requiring politically or economically unrealistic shock therapy.

## 5. Why This Can Pass Congress and Be Signed by President Trump

- **Alignment with Administration Priorities**: Growth via energy and tax relief; tariffs for revenue and leverage; aggressive efficiency against waste and bureaucracy; increases for defense and border security; no broad tax increases on Americans and no cuts to Social Security or Medicare benefits for current retirees.
- **Legislative Vehicles**: Portions fit reconciliation (tax, certain spending changes). Debt limit legislation provides a natural enforcement vehicle. Appropriations and continuing resolutions can incorporate discretionary elements and rescission packages.
- **Internal Coalition**: Delivers wins for growth advocates, fiscal hawks (via triggers and ledger), defense hawks, and those focused on government efficiency. State flexibility appeals to federalism-oriented members.
- **Political Framing**: "Protect what works, cut what doesn't, grow the economy so debt shrinks as a share of the pie." Visible tariff revenue and waste-recovery stories provide concrete offsets.
- **Precedents**: Builds directly on 2025 actions (tariffs, OBBBA elements, rescissions, DOGE initiatives) while adding the missing statutory teeth, transparency, and growth accelerators needed for durability.
- **Limited Bipartisan Openings**: Permitting reform, program integrity/improper payments crackdowns, and certain technical efficiencies have crossed aisles in the past or face low organized opposition.

The package is ambitious on execution but calibrated to what a unified or narrowly divided Republican government can deliver.

## 6. Risks, Named Assumptions, and Safeguards

**Key Named Assumptions** (to be validated annually in the Ledger):
- Real GDP growth of 2.5–3%+ sustained under the growth and energy provisions (with sensitivity tables for lower outcomes).
- Net tariff revenues after economic adjustments and any retaliation (monitor quarterly).
- Enforcement and rescission mechanisms achieving high fidelity (with clawback provisions if targets are missed).
- No major recession or external shock that invalidates baselines.
- Political sustainability of triggers and commission recommendations.

**Risks and Mitigations**:
- **Growth underperformance**: Sensitivity analysis and mid-course corrections required; tariff and efficiency components provide floor.
- **Legal or implementation challenges to tariffs/rescissions**: Build in fallback revenue or savings measures.
- **Entitlement commission gridlock**: Fast-track procedures and fallback triggers (e.g., automatic small parametric adjustments if no action).
- **Interest rate or inflation surprises**: Focus on primary balance and growth; debt limit triggers provide response mechanism.
- **Political erosion**: Strong transparency and public Ledger reporting create accountability.

Open problem (explicitly acknowledged): Long-term demographic pressures on entitlements will likely require larger adjustments beyond this package; the commission is the structured vehicle for addressing them.

## 7. Implementation Timeline (Illustrative)

- **2026**: Enact core legislation (reconciliation/debt limit vehicle) with immediate rescission package, regulatory budget, and tariff framework. Begin permitting reforms via executive and legislative action. Stand up Ledger reporting.
- **2027–2028**: Full implementation of growth measures (energy projects breaking ground). First efficiency targets and improper payment recoveries. Initial state Medicaid flexibility waivers. Commission delivers first recommendations.
- **2029–2030**: Mid-term validation and course corrections. Debt limit review tied to progress metrics.
- **Ongoing**: Annual Ledger reports with assumption testing and open-problem updates. Automatic triggers activate as needed.

## Conclusion

The national debt is a structural challenge that has accumulated over decades through a combination of demographic realities, policy choices, and insufficient enforcement of fiscal discipline. Past approaches have often relied on hidden assumptions, single-lever optimism, or failure to distinguish protected core commitments from adjustable elements.

The Fiscal Capacity and Stability Act applies a more rigorous framework: explicit classification and ledgers, capacity-aware partitioning, clean measurement of results, multiple complementary tools, named assumptions with validation, and growth as the exceptional layer that can outpace debt accumulation. It protects what must be protected for social and political stability while imposing real discipline on waste, overhead, and conditional growth in spending. It leverages tariffs and domestic energy/investment policy for revenue and expansion without new taxes on American families and businesses.

This is not a complete or painless solution—there is no such thing at current debt levels—but it is a credible, legislatively realistic package that can stabilize the trajectory, contain interest costs, and put the United States on a sustainable path consistent with strong economic growth and national priorities.

Congress and the President should enact it promptly, with rigorous implementation and annual honest accounting. The alternative—continued drift—is the least realistic option of all.
